## Part B - Survey Evaluation:

Identify and describe survey features:

```
**Sample Type**
The 2018 GSS on Giving, Volunteering, and Participating (Cycle 33) used a cross-sectional probability sample design. This means that participants were selected to represent the Canadian population at a single point in time, with each person having a known chance of being included. To ensure statistical validity and national representativeness, a stratified sampling approach was used, with particular focus on capturing differences between volunteers and non-volunteers.

**Sample Size**
Approximately 50,000 Canadian households were initially contacted for this cycle of the GSS. Of those, about 40,000 were invited to participate. Ultimately, the final dataset includes responses from approximately 20,000 individuals aged 15 and older. The large sample size ensures robust national estimates and allows for detailed subgroup analysis by province, age, gender, and other demographic variables.

**Target Population**
The survey targeted individuals aged 15 and older residing in private households across all ten Canadian provinces. This means that people living in institutions (such as nursing homes or prisons) and those in the three northern territories (Yukon, Northwest Territories, and Nunavut) were excluded. The focus was on understanding giving, volunteering, and civic participation across Canada’s general population.

**Sampling Frame**
The sampling frame was constructed from a combination of Statistics Canada’s Dwelling Universe File and telephone number databases (landlines and cellphones). This dual-frame approach allowed the inclusion of both traditional and mobile households, improving coverage and reducing bias from landline-only samples. The sampling frame was linked to postal codes to ensure geographic representation.

**Survey Mode**
For the first time in its history, the 2018 GSS allowed respondents to complete the survey either via telephone (Computer-Assisted Telephone Interviewing, or CATI) or online using a self-administered electronic questionnaire (EQ). This mixed-mode design aimed to improve accessibility, reduce interviewer bias, and increase response rates, especially among younger or digitally engaged populations.

**Timeline**
The data collection period for Cycle 33 ran from September 4 to December 28, 2018. This four-month window ensured sufficient time to reach a broad and representative sample of Canadians while allowing for follow-up attempts to improve response rates and data completeness.

**Response Rate**
While Statistics Canada did not publish an exact response rate in the public documentation, academic sources and internal analyses indicate a response rate of approximately 52.8%. This represents a moderate response rate, though slightly lower than previous GSS cycles (such as 2012, which saw over 65%). Declining response rates are a common trend across national surveys, but weighting and post-stratification are used to address potential biases.

**Weights**
Each respondent in the survey was assigned a survey weight to correct for the probability of selection and to ensure that the results are representative of the national population. These base weights were further adjusted using post-stratification by province, age, and sex to align with population counts from the 2017 Canadian Income Survey. Bootstrap weights were also provided for users conducting variance estimation and more complex statistical modeling.

**Data Processing**
Survey responses were processed through a standardized pipeline that included automatic edits within the CATI system and additional quality control checks at Statistics Canada’s head office. For online responses, the data were merged with CATI files and underwent the same cleaning procedures to ensure consistency between modes. This harmonization ensured that all responses followed the same logic and format for analysis.

**Cleaning, Imputation**
Although the documentation does not detail all imputation procedures, it is standard practice for Statistics Canada to conduct logical editing and imputation for key missing variables. This often includes methods like hot-deck imputation and consistency checks to ensure the internal validity of the data. Missing or inconsistent responses are flagged and corrected using established statistical rules to maintain the integrity of the final dataset.

**Sources of Error**
Several potential sources of error may affect the 2018 GSS data. These include sampling error, due to the inherent limitations of drawing from a subset of the population; coverage error, particularly among hard-to-reach populations; non-response bias, which may arise if certain demographic groups are underrepresented; and measurement error, caused by differences in interpretation or interviewer influence. The use of mixed survey modes could also introduce mode effects.

**Limitations, Known Biases**
There are several limitations to the 2018 GSS data. The exclusion of institutionalized individuals and residents of the territories means the findings cannot be generalized to those populations. The shift to include an online survey mode may also limit comparability with earlier GSS cycles that were conducted exclusively by phone. Additionally, changes in question wording or structure between cycles may affect trend analysis. Finally, while weights help correct for non-response, some biases may remain, particularly if certain groups systematically opted out.
